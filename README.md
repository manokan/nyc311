## NYC 311 Service Requests (aka complaints)

Random parsings of the public dataset about most things that ail NYC. I started delving into 311 data several years ago after repeated complaints to 311 about late-night jackhammer drilling across the street from me kept getting closed out with a comment like: "on inspection our inspector was unable to find the condition reported." This was construction that lasted several years; a lot of sleepless nights. I may never have learned R but for those sleepless nights!

At some point, I realized that public servants are accountable to the public only indirectly, via our elected officials. In the case of NYC services, that is the Mayor and members of the city council. Their runs for office are predicated on improving quality-of-life, but their performance is not measurable in the absence of any metrics. Is the council-person for a given district making a difference to their constituents? Committees of the city council have oversight over different departments. Is their oversight on the committee improving outcomes for the downstreams 'customers' of that department? This gave me the idea to remap all data to city council districts.

An incremental step from there: some months ago, the council member from the Upper East Side tweeted, in response to a noise visualization for his district, that perhaps the drop in noise in his district may be related to the evidence-based-governance policies he/his office were pursuing. That gave me the impetus to formalize what I was already doing into a 'governance-accountability' project. Abstract from the mountains of public data the metrics, the KPI, buried within. Put public data to the same uses that corporations do to their data - enable managers to improve efficiency given a set of constraints with the final objective of improving customer satisfaction. To flog that analogy a bit more - we are the shareholders and elected officials are the managers we 'hire' to run our city. 

The folders are organized by visualization subject and contain the aggregated data needed to run the code. The full, raw data files are [here at the NYC Open Data Portal](https://data.cityofnewyork.us/browse?q=311). Cleaning and parsing the roughly (at last count) 28 million rows is something I did incrementally over the months and years, and as needed for the [posts at DataTellsMe.Com](https://datatellsme.com). The code is a bit of a mess (understatement alert). I will be pushing it all here as I clean up the code. For now, *in media res*...
